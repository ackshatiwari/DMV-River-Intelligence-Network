# DMV River Intelligence Network


## What we do

Across river systems in the DC metropolitan area, such as the Potomac and its tributaries, water quality issues are often highly localized and transient—brief sewage overflows, storm-driven nutrient surges due to soil erosion, and runoff spikes can occur and disappear between traditional sampling intervals spaced out >6 months apart. Existing monitoring frameworks are sparse, slow, and infrequent, meaning that many of these short-lived, environmentally significant events are never properly recorded or understood at a fine spatial resolution. This lack of continuous, structured data makes it difficult to distinguish real contamination events from natural variability, limiting both scientific understanding and timely response.

To address this, the non-profit will construct a DMV River Intelligence Network, reframing river monitoring as a spatiotemporal graph learning problem rather than a monitoring platform. The river system is modeled as a Graph Neural Network, where nodes represent river segments and edges encode hydrological flow relationships between tributaries. Each node continuously aggregates multi-source signals, like turbidity, pH, conductivity, temperature, public environmental and weather data (rain, flow rates), and optional human validation inputs from local student chapters. Instead of treating chapters as operators of infrastructure, they function as distributed verification nodes that label, confirm, or reject flagged anomalies, creating a human-in-the-loop feedback layer for noisy real-world data.

On top of this graph, the system runs a multi-layer anomaly detection and consistency engine that goes beyond standard time-series ML. Local detectors identify sudden deviations, while a graph-based propagation model checks whether signals are hydrologically consistent upstream and downstream, reducing false positives from sensor drift or weather effects. A separate reliability model continuously scores each sensor and chapter input based on historical consistency and neighborhood agreement, effectively learning which data sources can be trusted over time. The final output is not a binary “pollution detected” signal, but a confidence-weighted environmental risk map of the DMV river network, enabling early warning, uncertainty-aware alerts, and structured escalation to environmental stakeholders.
